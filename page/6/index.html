一般来说的演算过程是

首先，我们需要明确我们要计算的是函数f(x) = x²在区间[0, 1]上的面积。

使用定积分的方法来计算该面积。

∫[0, 1] x² dx

= [x³/3]从0到1

= (1³/3) - (0³/3)

= 1/3 - 0

= 1/3

所以，函数f(x) = x²在区间[0, 1]上的面积为1/3

这个定积分是怎么算的我目前还没弄明白

但是我想要的，并且通过不断调整设问问chatgpt问出来的是这个

首先，我们将区间[0, 1]分成无穷小的小矩形，每个小矩形的宽度为Δx。然后，计算每个小矩形的面积，即x²乘以Δx。最后，将所有小矩形的面积相加，即求和Σ(x² * Δx)。

现在，我们来详细展开计算过程：

  1. 将区间[0, 1]分成n个小矩形，每个小矩形的宽度为Δx = (1 - 0) / n = 1/n。

  2. 计算每个小矩形的面积，即x²乘以Δx。对于第i个小矩形，其左边界为xᵢ = 0 + (i-1)Δx = (i-1)/n，右边界为xᵢ₊₁ = 0 + iΔx = i/n。小矩形的面积为f(xᵢ)Δx = (xᵢ)²Δx = ((i-1)/n)²(1/n) = (i-1)²/n³。

  3. 将所有小矩形的面积相加，即求和Σ((i-1)²/n³)。使用求和符号表示为∑((i-1)²/n³)，其中i从1到n。

  4. 展开求和符号：

    ∑((i-1)²/n³) = (0²/n³) + (1²/n³) + (2²/n³) + … + ((n-1)²/n³)
    = (0 + 1 + 4 + … + (n-1)²) / n³

  5. 计算求和：

    (0 + 1 + 4 + … + (n-1)²) = n(n-1)(2n-1)/6

    代入求和结果：

    ∑((i-1)²/n³) = (n(n-1)(2n-1)/6) / n³

  6. 化简：

    ∑((i-1)²/n³) = (n-1)(2n-1)/6n²

  7. 当n趋向于无穷大时，即n→∞，我们可以将Σ((i-1)²/n³)近似为定积分∫[0, 1] x² dx。

  8. 将求和结果代入：

    ∫[0, 1] x² dx = (n-1)(2n-1)/6n²

  9. 当n趋向于无穷大时，即n→∞，我们可以求出定积分的结果。

    lim(n→∞) [(n-1)(2n-1)/6n²] = 1/3

所以，函数f(x) = x²在区间[0, 1]上的面积为1/3

这个是我目前的能力能够理解的，并且它里面的第七部还使用了我之前学习的洛必达法则，所以当我发现我可以将之前学到的技能（我认为叫做“工具”更准确和熟练）付诸使用是一件微妙的事情。

所以从以上这些过程中可以看出很多的东西，第一不那么“广义和友好”的lecture需要一个非常庞大的已有的工具系统，这些工具系统的建立和熟练使用需要时间和脑力， 它的混沌程度粗糙的说和语言系统近似，在建立的时间尺度上可能和语言系统类似，可能一般以年为单位。我记得我完整地观看了（其实就是昨天刚刚看的）Wolfgang的采访，大约一个多小时，Wolfgang有提到在他32岁的时候，或者我记得他说的是，那个时候他已经32岁了，他不可能去从事全新的东西，他还是需要使用他原有的工具继续进行下去，所以我想对于他来说，也有很多工具是他所不熟练或者不会使用的。而我现在的局限性显然要强的更多了，不过我唯一的优势只是我还有能力选择学习和使用哪些工具。并且我我也在这个过程中个人可以做到的东西非常微小，后来我又认真的听了wolfgang在一个大学的对于他说研究的东西的简单介绍，大约一个多小时，如果不算结尾的提问时间大约是一个小时，于是迄今为止我也终于对AMO有了一个较为粗糙和系统的认识。

首先我可以感觉到Wolfgang所从事，接触思考的内容确实是与AMO，也就是atom，molecule，optic高度相关的，要想明白Wolfgang所从事的研究，最直接，深入和能够让人体会理解的切入性介绍就是低温，他要把一些物质降低到非常非常低的温度，然后通过介绍如何把物质降低到非常非常低的温度以及为什么要这样做，会有什么效果的过程中，他可以依次将所涉及的信息点有层次的剥离和介绍出来，于是他首先要告诉我们，什么是温度，以及要求我们终身都记住一个“有趣的事实”，空气分子正在以飞机的速度撞击你的皮肤，然后告诉我们如何降低温度，一种的激光的方法，这一种方法在上个世界大约90年代才逐渐开始普及或者流行起来，另一种和空调的方法一样。

采用激光，也就是光子，photon去撞击原子，然后通过多普勒效应将原子的能量逐渐带走（但是我还是不太明白多普勒效应是怎么把原子的能量带走的），当原子的能量逐渐减少，也就是它们的温度降低了，因为温度就是微观下分子的运动速度。然后Wolfgang告诉我们他将激光降温和压缩（原谅我不太记得那个术语的名称了）这两种结合在一起，并且这个结合花了他不少时间精力，因为激光降温的粒子群的温度上限和后者所要求的最低下限相差的很远，所以将两者较大的差距bridge在一起是一个以前没人做到过的东西。但是他做到了，具体怎样做到我好像不太记得，或者他没有具体解释？然后当温度降低到一个非常低的程度，低于宇宙中任何一处地方的温度的时候，当达到那个要求的时候，这些粒子就会出现波色爱因斯坦态，然后wolf刚从四个角度介绍了这一小团波色爱因斯坦态粒子的性质，我还有印象的性质是，这一小团原子，大约有百万级别的数量级的，所有的原子都以相同的方式运动，而一般的原子以无序的方式运动，并且它们有着特定的自旋方式，可以通过两束激光检测出来，并且它们存在特殊的衍射模式，因为它们同时具有波和粒子的性质。

July 11, 2023 Language, English

The market for tourism in remote areas is booming as never before. Countries all across the world are actively promoting their ‘wilderness’ regions - such as mountains, Arctic lands, deserts, small islands and wetlands - to high-spending tourists. The attraction of these areas is obvious: by definition, wilderness tourism requires little or no initial investment. But that does not mean that there is no cost. As the 1992 United Nations Conference on Environment and Development recognized, these regions are fragile (i.e. highly vulnerable to abnormal pressures) not just in terms of their ecology, but also in terms of the culture of their inhabitants. The three most significant types of fragile environment in these respects, and also in terms of the proportion of the Earth’s surface they cover, are deserts, mountains and Arctic areas. An important characteristic is their marked seasonality, with harsh conditions prevailing for many months each year. Consequently, most human activities, including tourism, are limited to quite clearly defined parts of the year. Tourists are drawn to these regions by their natural landscape beauty and the unique cultures of their indigenous people. And poor governments in these isolated areas have welcomed the new breed of ‘adventure tourist’, grateful for the hard currency they bring. For several years now, tourism has been the prime source of foreign exchange in Nepal and Bhutan. Tourism is also a key element in the economies of Arctic zones such as Lapland and Alaska and in desert areas such as Ayers Rock in Australia and Arizona’s Monument Valley. ￼ B Once a location is established as a main tourist destination, the effects on the local community are profound. When hill-farmers, for example, can make more money in a few weeks working as porters for foreign trekkers than they can in a year working in their fields, it is not surprising that many of them give up their farm-work, which is thus left to other members of the family. In some hill-regions, this has led to a serious decline in farm output and a change in the local diet, because there is insufficient labour to maintain terraces and irrigation systems and tend to crops. The result has been that many people in these regions have turned to outside supplies of rice and other foods. In Arctic and desert societies, year-round survival has traditionally depended on hunting animals and fish and collecting fruit over a relatively short season. However, as some inhabitants become involved in tourism, they no longer have time to collect wild food; this has led to increasing dependence on bought food and stores. Tourism is not always the culprit behind such changes. All kinds of wage labour, or government handouts, tend to undermine traditional survival systems. Whatever the cause, the dilemma is always the same: what happens if these new, external sources of income dry up? The physical impact of visitors is another serious problem associated with the growth in adventure tourism. Much attention has focused on erosion along major trails, but perhaps more important are the deforestation and impacts on water supplies arising from the need to provide tourists with cooked food and hot showers. In both mountains and deserts, slow-growing trees are often the main sources of fuel and water supplies may be limited or vulnerable to degradation through heavy use. ￼ C Stories about the problems of tourism have become legion in the last few years. Yet it does not have to be a problem. Although tourism inevitably affects the region in which it takes place, the costs to these fragile environments and their local cultures can be minimized. Indeed, it can even be a vehicle for reinvigorating local cultures, as has happened with the Sherpas of Nepal’s Khumbu Valley and in some Alpine villages. And a growing number of adventure tourism operators are trying to ensure that their activities benefit the local population and environment over the long term. In the Swiss Alps, communities have decided that their future depends on integrating tourism more effectively with the local economy. Local concern about the rising number of second home developments in the Swiss Pays d’Enhaut resulted in limits being imposed on their growth. There has also been a renaissance in communal cheese production in the area, providing the locals with a reliable source of income that does not depend on outside visitors. Many of the Arctic tourist destinations have been exploited by outside companies, who employ transient workers and repatriate most of the profits to their home base. But some Arctic communities are now operating tour businesses themselves, thereby ensuring that the benefits accrue locally. For instance, a native corporation in Alaska, employing local people, is running an air tour from Anchorage to Kotzebue, where tourists eat Arctic food, walk on the tundra and watch local musicians and dancers. Native people in the desert regions of the American Southwest have followed similar strategies, encouraging tourists to visit their pueblos and reservations to purchase high-quality handicrafts and artwork. The Acoma and San Ildefonso pueblos have established highly profitable pottery businesses, while the Navajo and Hopi groups have been similarly successful with jewellery. Too many people living in fragile environments have lost control over their economies, their culture and their environment when tourism has penetrated their homelands. Merely restricting tourism cannot be the solution to the imbalance, because people’s desire to see new places will not just disappear. Instead, communities in fragile environments must achieve greater control over tourism ventures in their regions, in order to balance their needs and aspirations with the demands of tourism. A growing number of communities are demonstrating that, with firm communal decision-making, this is possible. The critical question now is whether this can become the norm, rather than the exception.
